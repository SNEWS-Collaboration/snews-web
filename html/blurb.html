The SuperNova Early Warning System: Brief Description

When a massive star collapses at the end of its life, most of its binding energy is emitted in the form of neutrinos. These neutrinos come in all flavors and emerge promptly from the stellar envelope, over a timescale of tens of seconds. If the star later explodes, the burst of supernova photons does not become visible until hours laters. Therefore the observation of a neutrino burst can provide a warning for astronomers that the opportunity for a rare and valuable observation of the early turn-on of supernova light may be soon be presenting itself. A number of neutrino experiments with sensitivity to a gravitational collapse event in our Galaxy are currently online, or will be in the future. The list includes Super-Kamiokande, SNO, LVD, KamLAND, AMANDA, Borexino, mini-BOONE, and Icarus, as well as some proposed experiments such as OMNIS and LAND. In addition, gravitational wave detectors like LIGO and Virgo have sensivity to asymmetrically collapsing supernovae.

LVD (Large Volume Detector) at Gran Sasso National Laboratory in Italy.

The basic idea of the early supernova alert project is to have a central computer (or computers) which accepts neutrino burst candidate messages from neutrino detectors around the world and sends an alarm message to astronomers if it finds a coincidence within a short period of time (10 seconds). Currently, there are two SNEWS coincidence computers, one at the Super-Kamiokande site in Mozumi, Japan, and one at Gran Sasso. Super-Kamiokande and LVD are are online in "automated mode"-- alert messages from each experiment are sent immediately. SNO, KamLAND, AMANDA are also involved at different levels. The coincidence is "blind", i.e. is done without polling other experiments if one message is received.
